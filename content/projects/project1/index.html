Omega Group plc- Pay Discrimination

At the last board meeting of Omega Group Plc., the headquarters of a large multinational company, the issue was raised that women were being discriminated in the company, in the sense that the salaries were not the same for male and female executives. A quick analysis of a sample of 50 employees (of which 24 men and 26 women) revealed that the average salary for men was about 8,700 higher than for women. This seemed like a considerable difference, so it was decided that a further analysis of the company salaries was warranted.

You are asked to carry out the analysis. The objective is to find out whether there is indeed a significant difference between the salaries of men and women, and whether the difference is due to discrimination or whether it is based on another, possibly valid, determining factor.

You can also run a hypothesis testing, assuming as a null hypothesis that the mean difference in salaries is zero, or that, on average, men and women make the same amount of money. You should tun your hypothesis testing using t.test() and with the simulation method from the infer package.

H-null: difference in salaries between male and female = 0. H-alt: difference in salaries between male and female != 0.

# hypothesis testing using t.test()

diff_mean_salary <- omega %>%

# Generate null distribution
gender_salary_null <- omega %>%
  specify(salary ~ gender) %>%
  hypothesize(null = "independence") %>%
  generate(reps = 1000, type = "permute") %>%
  calculate(stat = "diff in means", 
            order = c("female", "male"))

# Visualize null distribution
gender_salary_null %>% 
  visualize() +

# Calculate p-value of observed difference

What can you conclude from your analysis? A couple of sentences would be enough

We can conclude that, at a 95% condidence level, the mean of male’s salary is different from female’s. There is a 95% chance that male executives earn between $4420 and $12973 higher than their female counterparts. There is a 0.0165% chance to see a difference of at least as large as $8696 between male’s and female’s pays. So, we can say that the null-hypothesis is untrue, considering we have a p-value below 0.5 and a red line considerably far from 0.

Check correlations between the data

You can use GGally:ggpairs() to create a scatterplot and correlation matrix. Essentially, we change the order our variables will appear in and have the dependent variable (Y), salary, as last in our list. We then pipe the dataframe to ggpairs() with aes arguments to colour by gender and make ths plots somewhat transparent (alpha = 0.3).

omega %>% 
  select(gender, experience, salary) %>% #order variables they will appear in ggpairs()
  ggpairs(aes(colour=gender, alpha = 0.3))+
